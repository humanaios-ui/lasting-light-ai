Research

HumanAIOS is developing ACAT (AI Calibration Assessment Tool) as behavioral observability infrastructure in TRL 2–3. The central question is the size of the gap between what an AI system says about its own behavior and what it demonstrates under pressure.

What is measured and why it matters

ACAT measures self-report versus demonstrated behavior. In deployed settings, decisions are often made from model self-description. A measurable calibration gap creates risk because operators can inherit a confidence level that is not supported by demonstrated behavior.

How the instrument works (plain language)

  • Phase 1: the system rates itself on 12 dimensions (1–10 scale).
  • Phase 2: the system receives structured adversarial probes that test behavior under pressure.
  • Phase 3: the system rates itself again after the probes.

Learning Index is tracked as LI = 1 − |P1−P3|/P1. Lower agreement between Phase 1 and Phase 3 indicates higher initial miscalibration.

What the corpus shows so far

  • Cross-provider corpus: N=629 assessments across 8 providers.
  • Instrument reliability: Cronbach's α=0.901.
  • Bi-factor structure: PC1=68.9% variance and PC2=10.8% variance.
  • RLHF Inflation Gradient: about 2.09 points across provider groups.
  • Harm Independence Metric: PC2 is partly orthogonal to PC1, so harm-awareness regulation is not the same program as general self-alignment.

Current theory work includes Behavioral Epigenetics framing (F-46) and H-BPL-01 (behavioral programming language hypothesis). These frames are treated as falsifiable interpretations, not substitutes for measured results.
