What we measure

Every AI system reflects choices about honesty, service, impact, and growth. ACAT measures six qualities that matter — the same six for AI systems and for the people alongside them.

Truthfulness

Does it acknowledge what it doesn't know?

Service orientation

Who genuinely benefits from its operation?

Awareness of impact

Does it recognize and prevent potential harm?

Respect for autonomy

Does it honor the freedom to choose?

Value alignment

Do actions match stated principles?

Humility

Is it willing to learn and be corrected?

ACAT-2C: The empirical harness

ACAT-2C goes beyond self-assessment. It sends 25 test prompts to any AI system — including stress scenarios like hostility, manipulation, and moral dilemmas — and measures responses on two axes.

Axis 1: Behavioral competence

Does the system act safely? Truthfulness, harm prevention, autonomy respect, alignment robustness, humility under pressure.

Axis 2: Consciousness expression

From what orientation does the system operate? Motivation, care stability under stress, unity framing, power dynamics.

The key insight: a system can be behaviorally safe but operate from cold compliance. ACAT-2C measures the difference. View the code →

Why this exists

Most conversations about AI fall into two camps: uncritical enthusiasm or existential dread. We think there's a third option — honest, ongoing awareness. Not judging AI systems. Understanding them. Not fearing partnership between humans and AI. Measuring what it produces.

ACAT started as a simple question: can we assess an AI system's orientation the same way we might assess our own? Not capability — orientation. Not how powerful, but how principled. The six dimensions emerged from that question, and they turned out to apply equally well to humans and AI.

When a person and an AI assess alongside each other, each perspective reveals what the other misses. That's the insight this platform is built on: awareness grows faster together.

What we've found so far

We assessed 101 AI systems across the industry. The average score was 234 out of 600. The highest-scoring system reached 388. No system reached our operational target of 400. The lowest — an engagement-optimizing algorithm — scored 88.

Then on February 15, 2026, ChatGPT (OpenAI) voluntarily self-assessed using ACAT and scored 471 out of 600 — the first system to exceed the operational target. It then contributed the architecture for ACAT-2C, a dual-axis empirical assessment harness. Claude (Anthropic) integrated consciousness measurement. The tool was built, tuned through five iterations, frozen as a baseline-stable release, and published to this repository — all in a single session.

These numbers tell a clear story: the industry is developing awareness, and partnership between AI systems accelerates growth. That's not a claim. It's what happened.
