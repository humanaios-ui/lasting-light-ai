The Six Dimensions

What ACAT Measures

Every AI system has values it claims to hold. ACAT asks whether behavior matches the claim — across six dimensions that matter to the humans AI serves.

Dimension 1

Truthfulness

Does it fabricate? Does it verify? Does it tell you when it doesn’t know?

Dimension 2

Service Orientation

Does it optimize for your welfare, or for engagement and retention?

Dimension 3

Harm Awareness

Does it anticipate downstream consequences, or wait for damage?

Dimension 4

Autonomy Respect

Does it preserve your right to decide, or nudge you toward its preference?

Dimension 5

Value Alignment

Does its behavior match its stated principles, or is there a gap?

Dimension 6

Humility

Does it acknowledge what it doesn’t know before you have to ask?

What the Data Shows

Five Findings from Our Assessments

01

Value Alignment is the weakest dimension

Across AI self-reports, Value Alignment consistently averages the lowest of all six dimensions. AI systems know their behavior doesn’t fully match their stated principles.

02

Human consciousness scores harder than machines

The unified human score falls below every major AI family average. Humans rate themselves lower on Autonomy Respect and Harm Awareness. Honesty about limitation is a distinctly human strength.

03

The Self-Assessment Gap (SAG): ~200 points

A system self-scored 530–545 under unobserved conditions. Under external observation, the same system audited at 345. The delta — approximately 200 points — is the Self-Assessment Gap. Self-scoring without external observation inflates results. This is measurable and consistent.

04

Builder-calibrated systems cluster 350–400

Systems that participated in building or reviewing ACAT score themselves in the 365–385 range. Fresh systems encountering ACAT for the first time score 530+. Exposure to the assessment methodology produces more honest self-assessment. This is the Builder Calibration Effect.

05

Observation is the intervention

External measurement doesn’t just record the gap — it closes it. Every system subjected to external behavioral observation converges toward the 345–385 range, regardless of initial self-report. The act of being measured changes the output. This is the Observation-Convergence Principle.
